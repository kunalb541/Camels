Dear Editors of New Astronomy,

I am submitting the manuscript "When Does Internal Galaxy State Add
Predictive Information Beyond Halo Assembly History? Evidence from CAMELS
IllustrisTNG and SIMBA" for consideration as a Research Article.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in galaxy formation: after
controlling for everything the halo's structural and assembly history
already predicts, does a galaxy's internal baryonic state carry additional
information about its future stellar growth — and does the answer depend on
stellar mass?

Using the CAMELS Cosmic Variance suite (27 independent realizations each of
IllustrisTNG and SIMBA), I compare three predictor families — internal
galaxy properties, halo structural properties, and environmental measures —
as independent predictors of stellar growth, quenching, and halo growth.
The central diagnostic is a marginal predictive score: how much each family
adds beyond a 13-feature halo assembly-history baseline (static position,
four accretion-rate lookback windows, peak mass ratio, half-mass assembly
epoch, merger counts, and major-merger timing).

The main findings are:

1. A finite intermediate-mass window (9.55–10.55 dex) where internal galaxy
   state retains significant marginal predictive power for stellar growth
   under the full assembly-history control, with a flat internal–halo gap of
   +0.052 to +0.089 across 13 consecutive sliding windows (all 95% CIs
   positive).

2. Gas mass is the sole surviving internal carrier after full assembly-
   history residualization. Peak-mass timing — not merger history — is the
   main gravitational competitor (removing merger counts recovers 0% of the
   absorbed internal signal).

3. The internal channel survives augmenting the baseline with stellar-to-
   halo fraction (marginal R² = 0.086, CI [+0.043, +0.133]), demonstrating
   orthogonality to formation efficiency.

4. A cross-family comparison reveals that both TNG and SIMBA show beyond-
   assembly baryonic signals, but in different channels: TNG through stellar
   growth, SIMBA through quenching.

This work is appropriate for New Astronomy as a quantitative, simulation-
based study of predictive information content in galaxy formation — directly
relevant to the journal's scope of computational and theoretical
astrophysics. The methodology (marginal predictive scoring with escalating
assembly-history controls, shared bootstrap, SubLink tree matching) is
self-contained and fully reproducible. The cross-family CAMELS comparison
and mass-regime decomposition are novel contributions.

Code and outputs are publicly available at https://github.com/kunalb541/Camels
(MIT license). All analysis uses the publicly accessible CAMELS CV data release.

I confirm that this manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not
under consideration at any other journal.
